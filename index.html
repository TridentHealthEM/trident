Neurology / Stroke Protocols
🧠
Code Stroke Protocol
Time SensitiveProtocolNeuro
▼
Goal: Tenecteplase (TNKase) within 45 minutes of arrival. Code Stroke can be called by any team member.

Activation Criteria

Any patient with neuro deficit symptoms with onset within 24 hours. Any team member (nurse, tech, APC, physician) can call Code Stroke.

Thrombolytic Agent

We use Tenecteplase (TNKase) — preferred over tPA due to ease of administration (single IV bolus). tPA is no longer used in this system.

Imaging

  • CT and CTA for all patients within 24 hours of symptom onset
  • CT Perfusion (CTP) at TMC/SMC only — CTP is not available at FSEDs
  • At FSEDs: obtain CT + CTA; if concern for LVO or intervention candidacy, discuss the case directly with the neurologist and neurointerventional physician

BP Management

  • Cardene (nicardipine) for BP control — start ASAP
  • Goal SBP <180 prior to thrombolytic administration

Tele-Neurology Role

  • Orders Tenecteplase if indicated
  • Places note in Meditech
  • Documents NIHSS

Thrombectomy (Code NET)

For large vessel occlusion (LVO): call the neurointerventionalist on call. If tele-neurology determines intervention is indicated, call a "CODE NET".
  • Code NET patients → admit to ICU

Other Rules

  • Fall + intracranial bleed → trauma consult after discussion with neurosurgery
TMC is a thrombectomy-capable Stroke Center and Primary Plus Stroke Center (DNV certified).

Dizziness Decision Tool — Triage Assessment
ProtocolTime SensitiveNeurology
▼
⚡ Activate Stroke Alerts within 5 minutes of arrival for any sudden onset dizziness within the last 24 hours with ANY of the following:
🚨 ACTIVATE CODE STROKE
  • Dizziness + Headache
  • Dizziness + Blurred vision
  • Dizziness + Change in walking ability
  • Dizziness + Recent head trauma
  • Dizziness + Numbness or Weakness
  • Dizziness + High BP
📻 RADIO FOR STAT TRIAGE
  • Dizziness worse when standing
  • Dizziness better when lying flat
  • Dizziness comes and goes

Step-by-Step Triage Flow

Step 1 — ASK: Are you able to walk normally?

Unable to walk at baseline → Activate Code Stroke
Able to walk at baseline → Proceed to Step 2

Step 2 — ASK: Does the dizziness come and go, or is it constant?

If it comes and goes or improves → ASK: Is it triggered by movement?
Triggered by movement → Not likely neuro related
No trigger → ASK: Any other symptoms? (headache, high BP, numbness, weakness → Activate Code Stroke; chest pain or passing out → not likely neuro)
If constant / nothing makes it better or worse → ASK: Any recent head trauma, unusual medications, or toxins?
Trauma or toxin present → Not likely neuro related
No trauma or toxin → Activate Code Stroke

TMC Level I Trauma Alert Criteria
Immediate ActivationProtocolTrauma
▼
ED MD must be in room by patient arrival for all trauma activations.
  • Glasgow Coma Score <13
  • Systolic BP <90 (child <1yr: <60–70; >1yr: <70 + 2×age in yrs)
  • Respiratory rate <10 or >29 (child <10 or >60; infant <20 if ≤1 yr)
  • Penetrating injury to head, neck, torso, or extremities proximal to elbow/knee
  • Flail chest
  • Two or more proximal long bone fractures or any open long bone fracture
  • Crushed, de-gloved, mangled, or pulseless extremity
  • Amputation proximal to wrist or ankle
  • Clinically apparent pelvic fracture
  • Open or depressed skull fracture
  • New onset of paralysis or suspicion of spinal cord injury
  • Active airway assistance post-traumatic event
  • Injury requiring blood products to maintain hemodynamic stability
  • Falls >20 feet in adults (1 story = 10 ft)
  • Burns: >15% 2nd/3rd degree, circumferential burns to extremity/torso, inhalation burns
  • Clinical Discretion
🚗
TMC Level II Trauma Alert Criteria
TraumaProtocol
▼
  • Falls >10 feet or 2–3× the height of a child
  • Intrusion >12 inches occupant side; >18 inches any side
  • Ejection (partial or complete) from automobile
  • Death in same passenger compartment
  • Vehicle telemetry consistent with high-risk injury (steering wheel deformity, starred windshield)
  • Pedestrian or bicyclist thrown, run over, or impact >20 mph
  • Motorcycle crash >20 mph
  • Burns: >10% TBSA 2nd/3rd degree not meeting Level I
  • Clinical Discretion
⚠️
Trauma Special Considerations
TraumaHigh Risk
▼
  • Age ≥55: Increased risk of injury/death
  • Age ≥65: SBP <110 may represent shock
  • Low-impact mechanisms (e.g., ground-level falls) may result in severe injury in elderly
  • Patients on anticoagulation with head injury → at risk for rapid deterioration
  • Children (<10 years) — use pediatric vital sign thresholds
  • Pregnancy >20 weeks with associated mechanism of injury
  • Alcohol or illicit drugs → evaluate with high suspicion for occult injuries

Sepsis Protocol
Time SensitiveProtocolSepsis
▼
Antibiotics within 15 minutes of sepsis recognition. 30cc/kg bolus based on Ideal Body Weight (IBW).

Core Steps

  • Use sepsis order sets — for nearly all conditions, have fail-safe mechanisms to avoid fallouts
  • Antibiotics within 15 minutes
  • Direct communication with RN
  • 30cc/kg IV fluid bolus (based on IBW)
  • Reassessment and documentation
  • Know the categories of sepsis
  • Discuss with hospitalist
  • Add-on order set as needed

Creatinine Testing & Contrast Media
RadiologyProtocol
▼

Risk Factors Warranting Baseline Renal Function Assessment

  • Age >60
  • History of renal disease: dialysis, kidney transplant, single kidney, renal cancer, renal surgery
  • Hypertension requiring medical therapy
  • History of diabetes mellitus
  • Metformin or metformin-containing medications (risk of lactic acidosis if AKI develops)
Patients without risk factors above do not require baseline creatinine before iodinated contrast administration.

Acceptable interval: up to 30 days for outpatients; shorter for inpatients, new risk factors, or heightened renal risk.

Teaching Physician Documentation & MIPS
ChartingComplianceResidency
▼

Recommended Attestation

"I personally saw and examined the patient. I have reviewed and agree with the resident's findings including all diagnostic interpretations, and treatment plans as written. I was present for the key portions of any procedures performed and the inclusive time noted in any critical care statement."

Key Rules

  • TP must provide face-to-face service and personally document it
  • TP must indicate review/agreement with resident's documentation
  • Resident documenting "attending was present" is NOT sufficient for billing
  • For time-based codes (critical care): TP must be physically present for the entire billed time period
  • For procedures: TP must be present for all key portions
  • For radiology interpretations: "read and agree" or "reviewed and agree" is acceptable
If TP fails to provide necessary attestation, the chart is not billable — even if resident's chart is complete.

Patient Experience: AIDET & Key Behaviors
Patient Experience
▼
Patient Experience = Perception minus Expectation. Evaluated on how you look, what you do, and how you say it — not clinical credentials.

Expected Interaction with Every Patient

  • 1. Introduce yourself
  • 2. Sit down
  • 3. Acknowledge patient/family
  • 4. Update patient, especially about delays
  • 5. Discuss findings
  • 6. Close encounter with plan of care

Communication Behaviors That Improve Scores (factor of influence)

  • Acknowledge wait and apologize — 16× improvement

$50-or-Less Discharge Medication Playbook
Cost AwarenessProtocol
▼
If the patient can't complete the discharge plan for ≤$50, it's not a real plan.

Universal Bundle (~$10–15) — Give to Almost Everyone

  • Acetaminophen ($4–6)
  • Ibuprofen or Naproxen ($4–6)
  • Famotidine or Omeprazole for GI protection ($4–6)

Infections

  • Cellulitis: Cephalexin OR TMP-SMX + NSAIDs. Avoid Augmentin unless truly needed.
  • CAP: Amoxicillin OR Doxycycline + Acetaminophen/Ibuprofen (≤$15)
  • Uncomplicated UTI: Cephalexin ± Phenazopyridine (≤$25)
  • STI (poor follow-up): Ceftriaxone IM + Azithromycin 1g PO in ED — treatment complete before discharge

Pain & MSK

  • Scheduled Ibuprofen + Acetaminophen ± Cyclobenzaprine (≤$20)
  • Ketorolac IM in ED for severe focal pain — no opioid Rx needed

Other Common Conditions

  • Asthma/COPD: Prednisone burst + Albuterol nebulizer solution (≤$40–50)
  • HTN starter: Amlodipine, Lisinopril, or HCTZ once daily (~$10)
  • DM Type 2: Metformin (~$10) | Insulin-required: NPH (Walmart) + syringes (~$25–40)
  • Anxiety/Insomnia: Hydroxyzine | Depression/Anxiety: Sertraline or Fluoxetine (≤$10)
  • Nausea/GI: Promethazine or Ondansetron + Famotidine or Omeprazole (≤$15)

Do NOT Prescribe (Breaks the $50 Rule)

  • DOACs, brand-name inhalers, combo antihypertensives, long opioid courses, meds requiring lab monitoring, "just in case" antibiotics
Documentation phrase: "Given the patient's limited resources and inability to ensure follow-up, the discharge plan was intentionally designed to prioritize affordability, simplicity, and completion of care."

Chronic Pain Care Plan Protocol
ProtocolImportant
▼
Follow the department chronic pain care plan for known chronic pain patients. Document clinical rationale for any deviation.

IV Opioid Limits

  • IV Fentanyl — max 50 mcg
  • IV Morphine — max 4 mg
  • Avoid Dilaudid (Hydromorphone) — not part of the chronic pain care plan

Antihistamines

  • ✅ PO Cetirizine or Hydroxyzine — acceptable
  • ❌ No IV or IM Benadryl (Diphenhydramine)

Adjunct Medications

  • Acetaminophen (Tylenol)
  • Gabapentin
  • Amitriptyline

Admission Policy

No admission for uncomplicated chronic pain. If there is no acute surgical or medical emergency, discharge with appropriate outpatient resources.
